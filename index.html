1. Introduction & Labour Market Status

🧠 I. What is Labour Economics?

Labour economics is the study of the functioning and outcomes of the market for labour. It is a branch of microeconomics that analyzes how workers are allocated to jobs and how employment decisions (hiring, quitting, compensation, career choice) are coordinated.

Key Analytical Perspectives

  • Behavioral Economics: Analyzing how employers and employees respond to general incentives like wages, prices, profits, and non-pecuniary aspects (working conditions).
  • Human Capital Theory: Studying the link between wages and investments in education and training.
  • Institutional Framework: Evaluating the effects of unions and social policies (minimum wage, active/passive labour market policies) on productivity and turnover.

⚖️ The Unique Specificity of Labour

In standard commodity markets (oil, wheat), the product is separate from the seller. In labour markets:

  • Labour cannot be separated from the worker. Labour services are rented, never sold.
  • Because the "service" (the worker's effort) is delivered in person, both the price (wage) and non-pecuniary conditions are crucial for the contract:

Not Just About the Wage!

Risk of Injury & Work Environment: A worker might demand a "compensating wage differential" for a dangerous job.

Flexibility & Fairness: Perceptions of fair treatment and management style directly impact productivity (Efficiency Wage Theory).

👥 II. Actors & Market Structure

The Three Key Actors

🧑‍🔧

Workers

🎯 Maximize Utility

They balance the trade-off between consumption (earned via wages) and personal leisure time.

🏢

Firms

💰 Maximize Profits

They demand labour up to the point where the cost of the last worker equals the revenue they generate (MRP_L).

🏛️

Governments

⚖️ Regulate & Equity

Influenced by political motives. They impose mandatory transactions (e.g., social security) that the market wouldn't naturally produce.

🏢 Labour Market Segmentation (Dualism)

Primary vs. Secondary Market

Primary Labour Market: High wages, stable employment, and "Internal Labour Markets" (ILM). In an ILM, firms hire only for "entry-level" jobs and fill higher positions via internal promotion, reducing hiring/training costs and information asymmetry.

Secondary Labour Market: Low wages, high turnover, and "precarious" contracts. There is very little mobility between the secondary and primary sectors (the "Dualization" trap).

🏛️ III. The Role of the State: Equity vs. Efficiency

Under "Ideal Conditions" (Perfect Competition), markets reach Pareto Efficiency. However, labour markets often suffer from Market Failures:

  • Imperfect Information: Firms don't know the true productivity of a candidate.
  • Externalities: Investing in a worker's training might benefit a competitor if the worker quits.

⚠️ The Equity-Efficiency Trade-off

Social policies (like unemployment benefits) improve Equity (fairness) but may reduce Efficiency by creating "work disincentives."

📊 IV. Labour Market Status: The Statistical Trap

The Labour Force Survey (LFS) categorizes the population, but these boundaries are fluid and can be misleading during a crisis.

🔢 Core Mathematical Formulas

\[ \text{Activity Rate (AR)} = \frac{E + U}{\text{Pop}_{15-64}} \times 100 \]
\[ \text{Unemployment Rate (UR)} = \frac{U}{E + U} \times 100 \]

🧐 Definitions & Hidden Realities

Employed vs. Unemployed

Employed: Includes those who worked at least one hour for pay during the reference week.
🚨 The Trap: You are "counted as employed" even if you are on temporary absence (sick leave, maternity) or working-time arrangements (short-time work).

Unemployed (ILO): Must be without work, available, and actively seeking.
🚨 The Trap: If you stop looking because you think there are no jobs, you move from Unemployed to Inactive, causing the UR to artificially drop.

🦠 V. Deep Dive: The Impact of COVID-19

During the 2020-2022 period, standard indicators failed to reflect the true scale of the disruption.

1. The Statistical "Invisibility" of Distress

  • Technical Unemployment (Retention): Governments used "Short-time work" schemes. People kept their contracts but worked zero hours. Statistically, they were Counted as Employed, whereas in a standard crisis, they would have been Counted as Unemployed.
  • The Discouraged Worker Effect: During lockdowns, job seeking became impossible or perceived as futile. Millions of people lost work but stopped searching. They were Counted as Inactive (Out of Labour Force) rather than Unemployed. This led to a "hidden unemployment" that didn't appear in the official UR.

2. The Inequality Gap (Disproportionate Impact)

The Crisis as an "Inequality Multiplier"

Gender Inequality: Women were overrepresented in "vulnerable" service sectors (hospitality, retail) and bore the brunt of increased "informal" household labour during school closures.

The Precarious Workforce: Workers on "Zero-hour" or temporary contracts were the first to be let go, as they lacked the protections of the primary labour market.

Low-Skilled vs. High-Skilled: High-skilled workers transitioned to Telework (retaining full wages), while low-skilled manual workers either lost their jobs or faced high health risks as "essential workers."

💡 Exam Tip : Measuring Health of Labour Market

If asked to evaluate the "health" of a labour market post-crisis, do not just look at the Unemployment Rate. Look at the Total Hours Worked and the Job Vacancy Rate. If UR is low but hours worked are also low, it indicates massive underemployment or "hidden" inactivity.
