1. Introduction & Labour Market Status

🧠 I. What is Labour Economics?

Labour economics is the study of the functioning and outcomes of the market for labour. It is a branch of microeconomics that analyzes how workers are allocated to jobs and how employment decisions (hiring, quitting, compensation, career choice) are coordinated.

Key Analytical Perspectives

  • Behavioral Economics: Analyzing how employers and employees respond to general incentives like wages, prices, profits, and non-pecuniary aspects (working conditions).
  • Human Capital Theory: Studying the link between wages and investments in education and training.
  • Institutional Framework: Evaluating the effects of unions and social policies (minimum wage, active/passive labour market policies) on productivity and turnover.

⚖️ Why is the Labour Market "Special"?

In standard commodity markets (oil, wheat), the product is separate from the seller. In labour markets, labor ($L$) as a factor of production has unique characteristics that make it "special":

  1. It cannot be separated from the human being. Labour services are "rented", never sold. The employer rents a person's time and effort along with their entire psychology.
  2. It is subject to numerous regulations. (Minimum wage laws, working hour limits, health and safety, right to strike, etc.) unlike the sale of a chair.
  3. Non-Pecuniary Properties: The contract does not stop at the wage. It includes the nature of the work, the level of responsibilities, and the physical and social work environment. Humans care about their conditions.
  4. Information Asymmetry: When hiring, the employer does not know the true productivity of a candidate, and the candidate does not know the true wage or long-term conditions over the employment spell.
  5. Importance for the global economy: More than half ($>50\%$) of national income is generated directly in the labour market. Furthermore, the vast majority of the population depends intimately on this market (whether they are working, looking for a job, or retired and depending on the contributions of active workers).

Not Just About the Wage!

A worker may demand a Compensating Wage Differential for a dangerous job, precisely because of these non-pecuniary properties.

👥 II. Actors & Market Structure

The Three Key Actors

🧑‍🔧

Workers

🎯 Maximize Utility

They balance the trade-off between consumption (earned via wages) and personal leisure time.

🏢

Firms

💰 Maximize Profits

They demand labour up to the point where the cost of the last worker equals the revenue they generate (MRP_L).

🏛️

Governments

⚖️ Regulate & Equity

Influenced by political motives. They impose mandatory transactions (e.g., social security) that the market wouldn't naturally produce.

🏢 Labour Market Segmentation (Dualism)

Primary vs. Secondary Market

Primary Labour Market: High wages, stable employment, and "Outfitnal Labour Markets" (ILM). In an ILM, firms hire only for "entry-level" jobs and fill higher positions via internal promotion, reducing hiring/training costs and information asymmetry.

Secondary Labour Market: Low wages, high turnover, and "precarious" contracts. There is very little mobility between the secondary and primary sectors (the "Dualization" trap).

🏛️ III. The Role of the State: Equity vs. Efficiency

Under "Ideal Conditions" (Perfect Competition), markets reach Pareto Efficiency. However, labour markets often suffer from Market Failures:

  • Imperfect Information: Firms don't know the true productivity of a candidate.
  • Transaction Barriers: Search costs, geographic distance, legal obstacles, and administrative burdens prevent workers and firms from finding each other efficiently. These frictions delay the matching process and create frictional unemployment even when suitable vacancies exist.
  • Externalities: Investing in a worker's training might benefit a competitor if the worker quits (positive externality not captured by the firm). Conversely, pollution from a factory affects workers' health without compensation (negative externality).
  • Public Goods: Job market information, vocational training infrastructure, and employment services are "public goods" — non-rivalrous and non-excludable. Because private firms cannot fully capture their benefits, the market under-provides them, justifying government intervention (e.g., public employment agencies, subsidized training programs).

⚠️ The Equity-Efficiency Trade-off

Social policies (like unemployment benefits) improve Equity (fairness) but may reduce Efficiency by creating "work disincentives."

📊 IV. Measuring LM: Essential Labor Market Statistics

To understand the labor market, it is crucial to master its fundamental statistics:

Definitions & The Population Formula

Size of Population ($N$) : The working-age population, typically aged 15 to 64.

Size of Labor Force ($LF$) : The active population. It is the sum of people who are working and those looking for work. $LF = E + U$.

Employed workers ($E$) : Those of working age who, during a given reference period, were engaged in any activity to produce goods or provide services for pay or profit. This comprises both self-employed and wage-employed workers, and is subdivided into:

  • (a) Employed persons "at work": Those who worked in a job for at least one hour during the reference period.
  • (b) Employed persons "not at work": Those temporarily absent from a job (e.g., illness, maternity leave, vacation) or due to working-time arrangements (e.g., short-time work schemes, furlough). Crucially, these are still counted as employed.

Unemployed workers ($U$) : According to the ILO definition, three strict conditions must be met simultaneously: (1) being without work during the reference period (not in paid employment or self-employment), (2) being currently available for paid employment or self-employment, and (3) actively seeking work (having taken specific steps to find employment).

LFPR vs ER (Participation vs Employment)

\[ \text{Labor Force Participation Rate (LFPR)} = \frac{LF}{Pop_{15-64}} \]

Identifies the fraction of the population (15-64 years old) that is "in" the labor market (those actively participating, either working or searching).

\[ \text{Employment Rate (ER)} = \frac{E}{Pop_{15-64}} \]

Reveals the exact proportion of the population (15-64 years old) that is actually engaged in productive employment.

🚨 The Gap: Economic Hardship

The differences between LFPR and ER highlight and quantify the presence of unemployment. During times of Economic Hardship, what happens?

  • Mathematical rule: both rates (ER and LFPR) fall. ER falls because people lose their jobs ($E \downarrow$). LFPR falls because people get discouraged and stop looking, exiting the Labor Force ($LF \downarrow$).
  • The gap widens: The drop in employment is always more violent and immediate than the exit from the labor force, which mechanically widens the gap between LFPR and ER.

  • Stock-Flow dynamics: Workers constantly flow between statuses — new entrants and re-entrants join the Labour Force, while dropouts and retirements exit it. Employed workers face layoffs and quits (→ Unemployed), while unemployed are matched to new hires (→ Employed).

📈 Long-Term Trends in Activity Rates

When looking at cross-country comparisons (e.g., Czechia vs. EU-28 average), key long-term trends emerge:

  • Female LFPR has steadily increased over the past decades, particularly for married women. This is one of the most significant structural changes in modern labour markets, driven by declining fertility rates, higher female education levels, changing social norms, and the expansion of the service sector.
  • Czechia consistently outperforms the EU average on both Employment Rate and Activity Rate, while maintaining one of the lowest Unemployment Rates in the Union.
  • The gap between male and female participation has narrowed considerably but has not fully closed, reflecting persistent structural barriers (e.g., childcare availability, gender pay gap).

🦠 V. Deep Dive: The Impact of COVID-19

During the 2020-2022 period, standard indicators failed to reflect the true scale of the disruption.

1. The Statistical "Invisibility" of Distress

  • Technical Unemployment (Retention): Governments used "Short-time work" schemes. People kept their contracts but worked zero hours. Statistically, they were Counted as Employed, whereas in a standard crisis, they would have been Counted as Unemployed.
  • The Discouraged Worker Effect: During lockdowns, job seeking became impossible or perceived as futile. Millions of people lost work but stopped searching. They were Counted as Inactive (Out of Labour Force) rather than Unemployed. This led to a "hidden unemployment" that didn't appear in the official UR.

2. The Inequality Gap (Disproportionate Impact)

The Crisis as an "Inequality Multiplier"

Gender Inequality: Women were overrepresented in "vulnerable" service sectors (hospitality, retail) and bore the brunt of increased "informal" household labour during school closures.

The Precarious Workforce: Workers on "Zero-hour" or temporary contracts were the first to be let go, as they lacked the protections of the primary labour market.

Low-Skilled vs. High-Skilled: High-skilled workers transitioned to Telework (retaining full wages), while low-skilled manual workers either lost their jobs or faced high health risks as "essential workers."

💡 Exam Tip : Measuring Health of Labour Market

If asked to evaluate the "health" of a labour market post-crisis, do not just look at the Unemployment Rate. Look at the Total Hours Worked and the Job Vacancy Rate. If UR is low but hours worked are also low, it indicates massive underemployment or "hidden" inactivity.
