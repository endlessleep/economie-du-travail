7. Education and Training

🎓 Is a university education a good investment?

The decision to attend university is fundamentally an investment decision. Individuals sacrifice current consumption and earnings (costs) in exchange for higher productivity and wages in the future (returns).

The Three Sources of Skills

🧬 1. Inherited Personal Characteristics

Before a student ever set foot in a classroom, they possess a "raw baggage" of intelligence, natural talent, and motivation. These traits are heavily influenced by the family environment—both genetically and through early childhood stimulation. This source of skill is crucial because it often determines the *efficiency* of future learning; naturally gifted or highly motivated students often gain more "human capital" from the same year of schooling than others.

📚 2. Skills Acquired via Formal Education

This is the visible part of the iceberg. Schools and universities provide structured environments to acquire technical knowledge (like coding or accounting), analytical frameworks, and social skills. In the Human Capital model, this process is seen as a direct transformation: the student "buys" schooling to increase their future marginal productivity, which the market will then reward with a higher wage.

💼 3. Skills Acquired via Experience (On-the-Job)

Learning doesn't stop at graduation. Most professional skills are acquired through "learning by doing"—the informal process of mastering tasks through repetition and exposure to real-world problems. This accumulates into a distinct type of human capital that is often highly valued by employers, as it proves the worker can translate theoretical knowledge into practical results.

🎓 Professor's Note: The Identification Problem

If we observe that university graduates earn 50% more than high schoolers, can we conclude that university *caused* the increase? Not necessarily. Highly talented people (Source 1) might be more likely to go to university *and* more likely to earn high wages anyway. If we ignore this "Ability Bias," we overstate the true return to education. Economists use twins or natural experiments to try and isolate the "pure" effect of schooling.

📈 The Economics of Schooling: Costs vs. Returns

Education is a long-term investment. To decide whether to enroll, we must compare the immediate costs with the discounted flow of future benefits.

1. Lifetime Earnings Profiles

Observations:

  • Level: Higher education levels lead to higher lifetime earnings.
  • Slope: Earnings profiles are steeper for highly educated workers (faster wage growth).
  • Peak: Tertiary-educated workers reach their peak income later (approx. 50-54 years old).

2. The Basic Investment Model

Cost Decomposition:

  • Area A: Forgone Earnings. The opportunity cost of not working during the study period.
  • Area B: Direct Costs. Tuition, books, and accommodation.
  • Area C: Earnings Premium. The net benefit (extra wage) gained after graduation.
Decision Rule: Go to university if the total gain ($C$) exceeds the total cost ($A + B$).

⚖️ Investment Decision Models

Since the costs occur now and the benefits occur over a lifetime, we must use Present Value (PV) to compare them accurately. A Euro today is worth more than a Euro in 10 years.

1. The Present-Value Method

We choose a discount rate ($r$) and calculate if the current value of future benefits exceeds the costs:

\[ PV_{Benefits} = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \frac{B_t}{(1+r)^t} \]

Rule: If $PV_{Benefits} > Cost$, the investment is profitable.

2. Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

The IRR is the specific interest rate ($r^*$) that makes the costs and benefits equal:

\[ \sum \frac{Costs}{(1+r^*)^t} = \sum \frac{Benefits}{(1+r^*)^t} \]

Rule: Invest if the $IRR$ is higher than the market interest rate.

🧠 Human Capital vs. Signaling

Does education cause higher wages (Human Capital), or is it just a filter (Signaling)? If we ignore innate ability, we attribute all wage gains to schooling—this is Ability Bias.

📉 1. The Signaling & Screening Model (Spence, 1973)

FIGURE A

The Threshold Effect: Employers set $e^*$. Degrees act as binary switches for wage levels ($W=1 \to W=2$).

🧠 The Logic of Signaling

  • ⚡ Asymmetric Information: A employer looking at a pile of CVs is like a blind person choosing fruit. They can't "see" your true productivity. They need a credible proxy—a signal—that you are indeed a "sweet" (high-productivity) worker.
  • ⚡ Visibility & Cost: For a degree to be a signal, it must be expensive enough that low-productivity workers won't "fake it." Crucially, this cost is not just tuition; it is the Psychic Cost of effort, stress, and time.
  • ⚡ The Cost Differential: The model only works if it is *harder* (more costly) for a low-ability person to graduate than for a high-ability person. If everyone could pass easily, the degree would lose all its value as a signal.

2. How to test HC vs. S&S?

🚀 The Self-Employed Test

A plumber who works for himself doesn't need to impress a boss with a fancy diploma. He only cares about his actual skills (Source 3) because that's what makes him money. If Signaling were the only reason people studied, we would expect self-employed people to have much less education than employees with the same income. In reality, they often have similar levels, which suggests that Human Capital (actual skill gain) is still a major factor.

🎓 The "Sheepskin" Effect

Why is the 4th year of university "worth" more than the 3rd? If education only increased skills (HC), the wage jump should be smooth. However, we see a massive "spike" in wages the moment you get the diploma (the sheepskin). This suggests the diploma is a credentialing signal—a finish line that proves to employers you have the "grit" and persistence to finish what you started.

💼 Job Training & Human Capital

Training is the continuation of human capital investment after formal schooling. It ranges from structured Formal programs to subtle Learning-by-doing.

Core Model: Wage vs. Productivity

Figure 7.2: Dynamics of the investment period ($W_0 < W_1$) and the return period where productivity reaches $W_2$.

🔵 Case 1: General Training

Imagine learning Excel or English. These skills are "General" because you can take them to *any* other firm. If your current firm paid for this training, you could immediately quit and get a higher wage elsewhere. This is the Hold-up Problem: firms refuse to pay for general training because they can't "capture" the return. Thus, it is the worker who pays, usually through a lower wage during the training period.

🔴 Case 2: Specific Training

This involves learning the proprietary software or unique "way of doing things" of your specific firm. Because these skills are useless elsewhere, the workers won't pay for it alone, and the firm won't pay for it alone (fear of you leaving). The solution? Shared investment. Costs and benefits are split to create a "mutual bond": the firm pays a bit more than $W_1$ and you stay to reap the rewards, making it a win-win.

💡 Lifecycle Fact: Training attendance declines with age as the "Return Horizon" shrinks.

⚖️ Social Rate of Return to Education

Governments must decide how many university places to fund. They look at Social Returns, which differ from private returns.

Social Costs

  • Public Funding: Staff salaries, infrastructure, running expenses.
  • Production Loss: The output society loses while the student is studying.

Social Benefits

  • Productivity Spillovers: Educated workers help their colleagues become more productive.
  • Innovation Diffusion: An educated workforce adopts new technologies faster.
🏛️ Why does the State pay for your degree?

If education only benefited the individual, there would be no reason for public subsidies. However, education creates Externalities (Social Benefits): an educated workforce is more likely to innovate, respect the law, engage in democracy, and share knowledge with colleagues (Spillovers). Since these benefits go to *society* and not just the *graduate*, the State pays part of the costs to ensure we don't "under-invest" in our future potential.
