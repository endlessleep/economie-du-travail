7. Education and Training

🎓 Is a university education a good investment?

The decision to attend university is fundamentally an investment decision. Individuals sacrifice current consumption and earnings (costs) in exchange for higher productivity and wages in the future (returns).

The Three Sources of Skills

🧬 1. Inherited Personal Characteristics

Before a student ever set foot in a classroom, they possess a "raw baggage" of intelligence, natural talent, and motivation. These traits are heavily influenced by the family environment—both genetically and through early childhood stimulation. This source of skill is crucial because it often determines the *efficiency* of future learning; naturally gifted or highly motivated students often gain more "human capital" from the same year of schooling than others.

📚 2. Skills Acquired via Formal Education

This is the visible part of the iceberg. Schools and universities provide structured environments to acquire technical knowledge (like coding or accounting), analytical frameworks, and social skills. In the Human Capital model, this process is seen as a direct transformation: the student "buys" schooling to increase their future marginal productivity, which the market will then reward with a higher wage.

💼 3. Skills Acquired via Experience (On-the-Job)

Learning doesn't stop at graduation. Most professional skills are acquired through "learning by doing"—the informal process of mastering tasks through repetition and exposure to real-world problems. This accumulates into a distinct type of human capital that is often highly valued by employers, as it proves the worker can translate theoretical knowledge into practical results.

🎓 Professor's Note: The Identification Problem

If we observe that university graduates earn 50% more than high schoolers, can we conclude that university *caused* the increase? Not necessarily. Highly talented people (Source 1) might be more likely to go to university *and* more likely to earn high wages anyway. If we ignore this "Ability Bias," we overstate the true return to education. Economists use twins or natural experiments to try and isolate the "pure" effect of schooling.

📖 Human Capital Theory

Gary Becker (1975) formalized the idea that education increases a worker's productivity. In his framework, education is not consumption — it is an investment in human capital. Just as a firm invests in machinery to produce more output, a worker invests in schooling to become more productive and, consequently, earn higher wages. The empirical regularity is clear: on average, more highly educated workers earn more than less educated ones.

📊 The Education-Wage Gradient: What the Data Reveals & Why

Empirical data from labour markets consistently confirms Becker's core prediction: wages rise monotonically with education level. A tertiary graduate earns roughly double a primary-educated worker. But the interesting question is not what the numbers show — it's why this gradient exists and what it proves.

🔵 The Human Capital Explanation

Under Becker's model, higher education increases marginal productivity. A graduate can solve more complex problems, learns faster, and coordinates teams more effectively. Competitive labour markets then translate this productivity advantage into a wage premium. The gradient is steep and persistent because the productivity gap compounds over a career — a highly educated worker not only starts higher, but accumulates further human capital faster via learning-by-doing.

🟠 The Signaling Counter-Narrative

Spence's alternative explanation says the gradient exists even if education does nothing to productivity. Employers simply cannot observe productivity directly, so they use the diploma as a screening device. High-ability workers self-select into education because it is cheaper for them — and this rational sorting creates the same wage-education correlation, without any causal mechanism. The gradient is real, but its interpretation differs entirely.

⚠️ The Gender Gap in Returns: A Structural Inequality

The data further reveals that education does not equalise wages between men and women: even at the tertiary level, women earn significantly less than men. This could reflect several mechanisms:

  • Occupational segregation: Women and men with the same degree tend to work in different fields and sectors — the feminised ones typically pay less.
  • Part-time penalty: Career interruptions for childcare reduce women's experience and human capital accumulation, flattening their lifecycle earnings profile.
  • Negotiation and discrimination: Even in identical roles, wage bargaining gaps and taste-based discrimination can explain a residual gap not accounted for by observable characteristics.
Key takeaway: The gender gap in returns to education is not a failure of Human Capital theory per se — Becker acknowledged discrimination exists. But it reveals that the labour market does not fully translate productivity into wages for all workers equally, raising equity concerns for public policy.

📈 The Economics of Schooling: Costs vs. Returns

Education is a long-term investment. To decide whether to enroll, we must compare the immediate costs with the discounted flow of future benefits. The key question is: is the sacrifice of 3–5 years of earnings and effort worth the lifetime of higher wages?

1. Lifetime Earnings Profiles

Figure 1: Average gross monthly earnings by education and age, CR, 2024. Source: Czech Statistical Office.

Three Key Observations from Figure 1:

  • Level Effect: At every age bracket (from <19 to 65+), higher education levels correspond to higher earnings. The gap between "primary" and "tertiary" is massive and persistent throughout the entire career.
  • Slope Effect: Earnings profiles are steeper for highly educated workers. This means their wages grow faster over time — they accumulate human capital more rapidly through experience.
  • Peak Effect: The earnings of tertiary-educated workers usually peak later in life (around age 50–54 in the CR in 2024), while vocational workers peak earlier and then plateau. This is because highly skilled jobs reward accumulated expertise more than physical stamina.

2. The Basic Investment Model

The decision at age 18: Going to university pays off if the lifetime wage premium exceeds total costs.

💸 Costs of Education

  • Area A — Forgone Earnings: The biggest cost is not tuition — it's the salary you didn't earn during 3–5 years of study. This is the opportunity cost: while you sit in a lecture hall, your former classmates are earning a full wage.
  • Area B — Direct Expenses: Tuition fees (where applicable), textbooks, supplies, and accommodation costs if the student must relocate to attend university.
  • Psychic Losses: Learning is often difficult and tedious. The stress of exams, the frustration of failure, and the mental effort required are real costs that affect well-being, even if they don't appear in a bank account.

📈 Expected Returns

  • Area C — Higher Future Earnings: The primary quantifiable return — the wage premium a graduate earns over a non-graduate for every remaining year of their career.
  • Nonmarket Benefits: Increased satisfaction over a lifetime, greater appreciation of culture, arts, and civic participation. These are real but hard to measure in monetary terms.
Decision Rule: Go to university if the lifetime gains from education exceed its total costs: $C > A + B$.

3. The Present Value Concept

Because costs occur now and benefits occur in the future, we cannot simply add them up. A Euro today is worth more than a Euro in 10 years because of the time value of money. We must discount future cash flows to their present value.

In 1 Year
$$FV: B_1 = B_0 \cdot (1 + r)$$ $$PV: B_0 = \frac{B_1}{(1 + r)}$$
In 2 Years
$$FV: B_2 = B_0 \cdot (1 + r)^2$$ $$PV: B_0 = \frac{B_2}{(1 + r)^2}$$
In N Years
$$PV: B_0 = \frac{B_n}{(1 + r)^n}$$
The HC Investment Rule: A rational individual takes a lifetime perspective. They compare the near-term investment costs ($C$) with the present value of expected future benefits ($B$). Investment in additional schooling is attractive if the present value of future benefits exceeds costs: $$\sum_{t=1}^{T} \frac{B_t}{(1+r)^t} > \sum_{t=0}^{S} \frac{C_t}{(1+r)^t}$$ Note: costs should also be discounted to PV — they are not one-time expenses but spread over the study period.

⚖️ Investment Decision Models

There are two complementary methods to measure whether the HC investment criterion is met. Both use the Present Value concept, but they approach the problem from different angles.

1. The Present-Value Method

The idea is simple: specify a value for the discount rate $r$ (typically the market interest rate), and then compute whether the present value of future benefits exceeds costs. If it does, the investment pays off at that interest rate.

\[ PV_{Benefits} = \sum_{t=s+1}^{T} \frac{B_t}{(1+r)^t} \quad \stackrel{?}{>} \quad PV_{Costs} = \sum_{t=0}^{s} \frac{C_t}{(1+r)^t} \]

Rule: If $PV_{Benefits} > PV_{Costs}$, the investment is profitable at rate $r$.

2. Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

Instead of choosing $r$, we ask: "How large could the discount rate be and still make the investment profitable?" We find the special rate $r^*$ that makes costs and benefits exactly equal, and then compare $r^*$ to the market interest rate.

\[ \sum_{t=0}^{s} \frac{C_t}{(1+r^*)^t} = \sum_{t=s+1}^{T} \frac{B_t}{(1+r^*)^t} \quad \Rightarrow \text{solve for } r^* \]

Rule: If $r^* > r_{market}$, the investment is worthwhile.

🇨🇿 Case Study: The 3-year Bachelor's Degree

This case study applies the Human Capital investment model to the Czech Republic context. The decision is made at age 18, with an expected working life to 65. The key economic insight is that the biggest cost of a university degree is not tuition — it's the salary you give up by not working during those 3 years. In the Czech Republic, where tertiary education is largely publicly funded, this opportunity cost (forgone earnings) accounts for roughly 90% of total costs. Direct costs — books, accommodation — are secondary.

On the benefit side, graduates earn a substantial and permanent wage premium over non-graduates. This premium compounds for the entire remaining career — roughly 44 years in this case. Even a modest annual premium, discounted back to the present, can dwarf the 3-year investment cost.

💡 Why Discounting Is the Core Economic Logic

The investment only looks attractive if you account for time preference: a unit of income today is worth more than the same amount in 10 years, because you could invest it at the market interest rate $r$. This is why the Present Value method is essential — it converts all future cash flows into a common comparable unit (today's money). At a low discount rate (e.g. 1%), future benefits are barely penalised, so the long-run wage premium easily justifies the upfront cost. At a high discount rate (e.g. 10%), the distant future benefits are heavily discounted and may no longer cover the immediate sacrifice.

Economic interpretation: Students from lower-income households often face higher effective discount rates — they cannot afford to wait for returns, or they face credit constraints that prevent them from borrowing to finance their studies. This is why free or subsidised education dramatically increases university attendance among disadvantaged groups: it eliminates the biggest cost (tuition) and reduces the effective investment barrier.

💡 What does 4.8% mean economically? It means the investment in education is equivalent to depositing money in a bank account that yields 4.8% per year — guaranteed, for life. Since the actual cost of borrowing (market rate) is only 1%, the investment is clearly profitable. The IRR also tells us how resilient the investment is: even if market interest rates rose to 4%, education would still pay off. Only above 4.8% would the investment become unprofitable — meaning the future earnings premium would no longer be worth the upfront sacrifice.

🔮 Predictions of the Theory

One of the most powerful aspects of Human Capital theory is that it generates testable, falsifiable predictions about observable behaviour. These are not just theoretical curiosities — they describe regularities we actually observe in real labour markets.

1. Students are Young

The model predicts that education is front-loaded in the lifecycle. The younger you invest, the more years $(T - s)$ remain to collect the wage premium. A 40-year-old going to university has only 25 years of returns to recoup a 3-year cost — the maths rarely works. This explains why adult education rates are low despite often high private motivation, and why governments pushing "lifelong learning" face real structural barriers rooted in investment logic, not just preferences.

2. Costs ↑ → Attendance ↓

Any increase in the cost of education — tuition fees, reduced student grants, higher interest on student loans — shifts the PV(Costs) upward, potentially making the investment unprofitable. The distributive consequence is critical: credit-constrained students from low-income families cannot borrow against their future earnings, so they face an effective exclusion from higher education that has nothing to do with ability. This justifies grants, free tuition, and income-contingent loan repayment schemes.

3. Wage Gap ↑ → Attendance ↑

When the graduate-non-graduate wage premium rises, the expected return to education increases, attracting more students. This explains the surge in university enrollment observed in the US since the 1980s, as skill-biased technological change (computers, automation) massively increased the demand for skilled workers and widened the college wage gap. The model thus links macroeconomic forces — technology, globalisation — directly to individual educational decisions.

⚠️ Problems of Estimating Returns to Education

Market Responses (Flooding Effect)

High returns to tertiary education attract more students today. But as these graduates flood the labour market, market forces will lower the returns to tertiary education in the future. This creates uncertainties about expected payoffs — current estimates of future returns may be unrealistic because they don't account for the supply response.

Ability Bias (Overestimation)

The role of education is overestimated because it is hard to distinguish between the contribution of ability and of education to higher earnings. Some people with degrees would earn more even without a degree — their innate talent drives both outcomes. Moreover, socioeconomic family background is correlated with both attained education level and future earnings.

Empirical Evidence (Twins Studies): Economists study identical twins where one went to university and the other didn't, to separate ability from education. Results are mixed: the proportion of returns to education accounted to family background and ability ranges from very low to about 1/3.

🧠 Human Capital vs. Signaling

Human Capital theory (HC) says an additional year of education increases your productivity — and therefore your wage. Screening & Signaling hypothesis (S&S) by Michael Spence (1973) says the opposite: educational systems serve as a means to find out who is productive, and do not increase individuals' productivity. Education is just a filter.

📉 1. The Signaling & Screening Model (Spence, 1973)

FIGURE A

The Threshold Effect: Employers set $e^*$. Degrees act as binary switches for wage levels ($W=1 \to W=2$).

🧠 The Logic of Signaling

  • ⚡ Asymmetric Information: A employer looking at a pile of CVs is like a blind person choosing fruit. They can't "see" your true productivity. They need a credible proxy—a signal—that you are indeed a "sweet" (high-productivity) worker.
  • ⚡ Visibility & Cost: For a degree to be a signal, it must be expensive enough that low-productivity workers won't "fake it." Crucially, this cost is not just tuition; it is the Psychic Cost of effort, stress, and time.
  • ⚡ The Cost Differential: The model only works if it is *harder* (more costly) for a low-ability person to graduate than for a high-ability person. If everyone could pass easily, the degree would lose all its value as a signal.

📐 Reading the Signaling Graph

This graph illustrates the Spence signaling model. The key idea is not that education increases productivity, but that it signals pre-existing ability to employers.

📊 What the graph shows
  • The horizontal axis represents years of education beyond high school.
  • The vertical axis represents wages.
  • The wage stays constant at 1 up to a threshold $e^*$.
  • Once education reaches $e^*$, the wage jumps to 2 and stays there.
⚡ The step-function logic
  • If $e < e^*$ → wage = 1
  • If $e \geq e^*$ → wage = 2
  • Wages do not increase gradually with education — they jump only once the signal becomes credible.
The Separating Equilibrium: Employers set $e^*$ as a threshold because they cannot directly observe worker ability. The threshold works as a natural filter:
  • Low-ability workers face a high education cost — reaching $e^*$ costs more than the wage gain of 1. They rationally choose 0 education and receive wage = 1.
  • High-ability workers face a lower education cost — reaching $e^*$ costs less than the wage gain of 1. They invest exactly $e^*$ years and receive wage = 2.

Conclusion: Education acts as a signal of ability, not necessarily as a source of productivity. Schooling attainment allows employers to separate high-ability from low-ability workers — even if the actual learning is irrelevant. Under this hypothesis, the social rate of return to education is misleading because there is no causal link between education and productivity.

Separating Equilibrium Analysis

📍 Separating Equilibrium Analysis ($C$ vs. $C/2$)

Group 1 (Low-Ability):
  • Productivity = 1
  • Cost of each year of education = C
  • Will signal only if the wage gain exceeds the signaling cost: $1 > C \cdot e^*$ (i.e., $e^* < \frac{1}{C}$).
Group 2 (High-Ability):
  • Productivity = 2
  • Cost of each year of education = C/2 (learn faster)
  • Will signal only if the wage gain exceeds the signaling cost: $1 > \frac{C}{2} \cdot e^*$ (i.e., $e^* < \frac{2}{C}$).
Conclusion: If employers set $e^*$ such that $\frac{1}{C} < e^* < \frac{2}{C}$, then low-ability workers find it too expensive and choose 0 years of education (wage = 1), while high-ability workers choose exactly $e^*$ years (wage = 2). The schooling attainment perfectly signals productivity.

2. How to test HC vs. S&S empirically?

Empirical testing is not easy — some attempts have produced mixed evidence. Here are the key tests economists have designed:

🎓 University Dropouts

Those who enter but do not finish university. Under HC, they should still have higher productivity (they learned something). Under S&S, they have no signal (no diploma), so no wage premium. If dropouts still earn more than non-attendees, this supports HC.

📚 School Quality

Under HC, attending a higher-quality school (better teachers, resources) should lead to higher productivity and wages because you actually learn more. Under S&S, school quality should matter less because the diploma is the signal, not the content learned.

🚀 The Self-Employed Test

A plumber who works for himself doesn't need to impress a boss with a fancy diploma. Under S&S, self-employed should acquire lower educational levels since they don't need the signal. In reality, they often have similar levels, which suggests that Human Capital (actual skill gain) is still a major factor.

🔬 Field of Study

Under HC, workers who study the field they actually work in should earn higher wages (their education directly boosted their job-specific productivity). Under S&S, there should be no difference in wages according to the subject studied — the diploma itself is all that matters.

🎓 The "Sheepskin" Effect

Why is the final year of university "worth" more than the 3rd? If education only increased skills (HC), the wage jump per year should be smooth. However, we see a massive spike in wages the moment you get the diploma (the sheepskin). This supports S&S: the diploma is a credentialing signal — a finish line that proves to employers you have the persistence and ability to complete the degree.

Why the Logarithm of Wages?

Statistical reason: Wage distribution is typically right-skewed (many people earn below-average and only few earn very high wages). Taking $\ln(W)$ brings the distribution closer to normal, making OLS regression more reliable.

Interpretation advantage: When the dependent variable is in $\ln$, we can roughly interpret the coefficients as a % increase in wage per one-unit increase in education (ceteris paribus).

Proper Interpretation: $e^{\beta}$ shows the % increase of the geometric (not arithmetic) mean of wages. With a one-unit increase of education, we expect to see $e^{\beta}$ times higher geometric mean wage.

Worked Example: Coefficient of sec_educ = 0.3827
$e^{0.3827} = 1.466$
→ Secondary educated had on average 1.466 times higher wages (geometric mean) than those with primary education → +46.6%

📐 What does β₁ ≈ 8–10% tell us about the labour market?

The Mincer coefficient on schooling, consistently estimated at 8–10% per year of education across many countries and time periods, has been called one of the most robust regularities in empirical economics. But what does it mean?

  • Under Human Capital theory: Each additional year of schooling raises a worker's marginal productivity by ~8–10%, and competitive labour markets translate this into a proportional wage increase. The market is therefore rewarding real, accumulated skill.
  • Under Signaling theory: The coefficient captures the sorting effect — more years of school select for higher-ability individuals. The premium is real, but it's the ability that's valued, not the schooling per se.
  • Under Ability Bias: The OLS estimate of β₁ is likely upward biased. Because more talented individuals both earn more and choose more schooling, the regression conflates two effects. Instrumental variable methods (e.g., using distance to university as an instrument) typically yield smaller, but still positive, estimates.

Why Experience Squared? (Concavity Proof)

Using Czech OLS estimates as an example:

$$\widehat{\ln W} = \alpha + 0.0264E - 0.0005E^2 + \cdots$$

Taking the first derivative with respect to $E$:

$$\frac{\partial \ln W}{\partial E} = 0.0264 - 2 \times 0.0005 \cdot E = 0.0264 - 0.001E$$
  • For $E < 26.4$, the function is increasing (wages rise with experience)
  • For $E > 26.4$, the function is decreasing (wages start declining — diminishing returns)

Second derivative: $\frac{\partial^2 \ln W}{\partial E^2} = -0.001 < 0$ → Concave (bell-shaped earnings profile).

💡 Economic intuition behind the concavity: Early in a career, each additional year of experience generates large productivity gains — a junior employee learning fast, mastering new skills, taking on more complex projects. But by mid-career, the marginal return to another year of experience falls. The worker has already mastered the core competencies; incremental years add less. Eventually, productivity may actually decline slightly due to technological obsolescence (skills become outdated) or physical factors. The quadratic specification $E^2$ is a simple and elegant way to capture this natural S-shaped lifecycle without additional parameters.

💼 Job Training & Human Capital

Training is the continuation of human capital investment after formal schooling. It occurs through formal training programs, learning by doing (skills improving with practice), and informally by working with a more experienced colleague. The central question is not just whether training creates value, but who pays for it — because the answer depends entirely on the transferability of the skills acquired.

🔍 The Training Investment Problem

Unlike a physical machine, human capital walks out the door every evening. This creates a fundamental asymmetry: a firm that invests in training a worker risks losing that investment if the worker is poached by a competitor who can offer a higher wage. This "hold-up" problem is the key tension in training economics, and it has very different resolutions depending on whether the skills are general or specific.

During training, the worker is less productive (part of their time is spent learning, not producing). This lost output is the real cost of training. After training, the worker's productivity — and potentially their wage — rises. The distribution of this surplus between worker and firm is the central question.

  • Costs = lost output during the training period (Area ABCD in the figure)
  • Returns = higher post-training productivity (Area EFGB in the figure)

Costs = Area ABCD (lost output during training). Returns = Area EFGB (higher post-training productivity).

🔵 Case 1: General Training

Skills transferable across firms (e.g., driving, statistical software, languages). The worker has a higher productivity of $W_2$ and can apply for it in a wide range of firms. If the current employer doesn't pay $W_2$, the worker simply leaves. Therefore, the employer shifts all costs to the worker by paying $W_0$ during training. Wage profile: DCEF. Costs paid entirely by the employee.

🔴 Case 2: Specific Training

Skills useful only in the current firm (proprietary software, internal workflows). The worker's productivity in any other firm won't be higher (would get $W_1$ elsewhere). Seemingly, the firm could pay $W_1$ forever (profile ABG) and bear all costs. But the worker has strong bargaining power: if they leave, the employer loses the entire investment ABCD. Solution: Wage profile HBK — costs and benefits are shared. Both parties have an incentive to prolong employment.

🔄 Why Specific Training Creates a Bilateral Monopoly

In the specific training case, both the firm and the worker become mutually dependent on the continuation of their relationship. After training, the worker is worth $W_2$ to the current firm but only $W_1$ to all other firms (the skill doesn't transfer). The current firm could theoretically pay $W_1$ forever and capture all the surplus — but the worker knows they are producing $W_2$ and can threaten to leave. Conversely, the firm knows the worker is stuck (no other employer values the skill at $W_2$), so it won't be outbid.

The solution is a shared investment: the firm pays part of the training cost (by accepting $W_0$ slightly above what it needs to), and the worker accepts a wage above $W_1$ but below $W_2$ after training. Both parties have "skin in the game" — which reduces voluntary turnover and explains why long-tenure relationships are more common in sectors with high specific training (e.g., banking, manufacturing).

💡 Lifecycle Fact: Training attendance declines with age as the "Return Horizon" shrinks — exactly as the HC model predicts. Firms rationally avoid investing in training older workers because the post-training period over which they can recover costs is shorter. This creates a tension with ageing workforce policies: incentivising firms to train senior workers requires subsidies precisely because the market under-provides this investment.

⚖️ Social Rate of Return to Education

Governments must decide if the expected social benefits of increased productivity outweigh the opportunity costs of investing more social resources in the educational sector. Again, the present-value method or the internal rate of return method can be used.

Social Costs

  • Opportunity costs: The loss to society of the potential output during the studies of the student (they could have been producing goods/services).
  • Direct costs of operating universities: Staff salaries, running expenses, infrastructure — all funded by the government at state universities.

Social Benefits

  • Greater outputs: Returns can be measured as the difference between earnings of the graduate and the non-graduate.
  • Positive externalities: Higher education of one worker positively stimulates productivity of co-workers; in a more educated workforce, innovations can be spread more quickly.

Two Extra Assumptions (not needed for private returns)

When computing social returns, two additional assumptions are required that were not necessary for calculating private returns:

  1. Earnings approximate marginal productivity — i.e., the wage reflects the true social value of the worker's output.
  2. Higher marginal product is caused by the extra education received — this is the core assumption of Human Capital theory. If S&S is correct, this assumption fails and the social returns are overstated.
🏛️ Why does the State pay for your degree?

If social benefits are larger than private benefits (due to positive externalities like innovation diffusion and productivity spillovers), then the social rate of return exceeds the private rate, justifying public subsidies. However, others claim that the returns to society are smaller than expected because education does not increase productivity (the S&S critique) — it's just an expensive sorting mechanism.

📊 Rate of return to training: Can be computed in the same way as for education — compare the present value of costs (lost output during training) and benefits (higher post-training productivity).
