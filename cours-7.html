7. Education and Training

🎓 Is a university education a good investment?

The decision to attend university is fundamentally an investment decision. Individuals sacrifice current consumption and earnings (costs) in exchange for higher productivity and wages in the future (returns).

The Three Sources of Skills

🧬

1. Inherited Traits

Intelligence, talent, and motivation developed within the family before and during schooling.

📚

2. Formal Education

Acquiring knowledge, technical skills, and analytical abilities through schools and universities.

💼

3. Work Experience

Skills acquired through "learning by doing" and informal training on the job.

Identification Problem: Do high wages come from education, or do highly intelligent people simply study more and earn more anyway? (Correlation vs. Causality).

📈 The Economics of Schooling: Costs vs. Returns

Education is a long-term investment. To decide whether to enroll, we must compare the immediate costs with the discounted flow of future benefits.

1. Lifetime Earnings Profiles

Observations:

  • Level: Higher education levels lead to higher lifetime earnings.
  • Slope: Earnings profiles are steeper for highly educated workers (faster wage growth).
  • Peak: Tertiary-educated workers reach their peak income later (approx. 50-54 years old).

2. The Basic Investment Model

Cost Decomposition:

  • Area A: Forgone Earnings. The opportunity cost of not working during the study period.
  • Area B: Direct Costs. Tuition, books, and accommodation.
  • Area C: Earnings Premium. The net benefit (extra wage) gained after graduation.
Decision Rule: Go to university if the total gain ($C$) exceeds the total cost ($A + B$).

⚖️ Investment Decision Models

Since the costs occur now and the benefits occur over a lifetime, we must use Present Value (PV) to compare them accurately. A Euro today is worth more than a Euro in 10 years.

1. The Present-Value Method

We choose a discount rate ($r$) and calculate if the current value of future benefits exceeds the costs:

\[ PV_{Benefits} = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \frac{B_t}{(1+r)^t} \]

Rule: If $PV_{Benefits} > Cost$, the investment is profitable.

2. Internal Rate of Return (IRR)

The IRR is the specific interest rate ($r^*$) that makes the costs and benefits equal:

\[ \sum \frac{Costs}{(1+r^*)^t} = \sum \frac{Benefits}{(1+r^*)^t} \]

Rule: Invest if the $IRR$ is higher than the market interest rate.

🧠 Human Capital vs. Signaling

Does education cause higher wages (Human Capital), or is it just a filter (Signaling)? If we ignore innate ability, we attribute all wage gains to schooling—this is Ability Bias.

1. The Signaling & Screening Model (Spence, 1973)

In this framework, education serves as a signal to help employers distinguish between high-productivity and low-productivity workers under uncertainty.

The Wage Schedule: Employers set a threshold $e^*$. If you have the degree ($e \geq e^*$), you earn a high wage ($W=2$); otherwise, you earn $W=1$.

🔑 Core Mechanism

  • Problem: Firms can't see true productivity.
  • Signal: Education (years of schooling) is used as a proxy.
  • Cost condition: For a signal to be effective, it must be cheaper for high-ability people to acquire than for low-ability people.

📍 The Separating Equilibrium

Education perfectly separates the two types if:

  • Low-Ability ($C$): The cost of $e^*$ is higher than the wage gain ($W_2 - W_1$). They choose 0 education (Point A).
  • High-Ability ($C/2$): The cost of $e^*$ is lower than the wage gain. They choose $e^*$ (Point D).
Social Return: In extreme signaling models, social return is near zero because education reveals but doesn't create productivity.

2. How to test HC vs. S&S?

🚀 Self-Employed

They don't need to signal to an employer. If S&S holds, they should have lower education levels than employees for the same income.

🎓 Sheepskin Effect

Do you get a wage jump for the year you graduate vs. the year before? (S&S: Huge jump; HC: Smooth increase).

📉 The Mincer Earnings Function

The standard empirical tool to measure the return to education and experience. It uses a semi-log specification to linearize the relationship.

\[ \ln W_i = \alpha + \beta_1 S_i + \beta_2 E_i + \beta_3 E_i^2 + \varepsilon_i \]

🔑 Key components

  • $\ln W$: Log wages (approx. normal distribution).
  • $\beta_1$: The Return to Education (e.g., +8% per year).
  • $E, E^2$: Captured the Concave lifetime profile (diminishing returns to experience).

📊 Numerical Interpretation

If $\ln W = \alpha + 0.0264E - 0.0005E^2$:

  • Wages increase for the first 26 years.
  • The peak occurs when $\frac{\partial \ln W}{\partial E} = 0$.
  • Interpretation: $100 \cdot (e^{\beta} - 1)\%$ increase.
Experience Proxy: When actual experience isn't available, we use Potential Experience: $E = Age - S - 6$.

💼 Job Training & Human Capital

Training is the continuation of human capital investment after formal schooling. It can be Formal (programs), Informal (learning by doing), or Social (mentoring).

💸 Who pays for training?

The answer depends on whether the skills are transferable to other firms.

🔵 Case 1: General Training

Skills useful in many firms (e.g., Excel, English). The worker pays via lower wages ($W_0$) during training, as the firm cannot "lock in" the productivity gain ($W_2$).

🔴 Case 2: Specific Training

Skills useful only in the current firm. Costs and benefits are shared. Employers pay higher than $W_1$ to reduce turnover and protect their investment.

Observation: Training attendance typically declines with age because older workers have a shorter time horizon to recoup the investment costs.

⚖️ Social Rate of Return to Education

Governments must decide how many university places to fund. They look at Social Returns, which differ from private returns.

Social Costs

  • Public Funding: Staff salaries, infrastructure, running expenses.
  • Production Loss: The output society loses while the student is studying.

Social Benefits

  • Productivity Spillovers: Educated workers help their colleagues become more productive.
  • Innovation Diffusion: An educated workforce adopts new technologies faster.
Conclusion: If there are positive Externalities (benefits to others), the social rate of return exceeds the private rate, justifying public subsidies for higher education.
