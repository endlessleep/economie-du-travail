The Microeconomics of Labour Demand

⏱️ I. Short-Run Labour Demand (SRLD)

In the short run, capital ($K$) is fixed. The firm can only vary the amount of labour ($L$) it employs to adjust its production.

1. The Decision Rule: Marginal Analysis

The Golden Rule of Hiring

A firm maximizes profit by comparing the cost of an additional worker to the revenue that specific worker generates. It's a pure cost-benefit analysis at the margin.

  • Marginal Revenue Product ($MRP_L$): The additional revenue from hiring one more worker ($MRP_L = MP_L \times MR$).
  • Marginal Cost of Labour ($MC_L$): The additional cost of hiring one more worker. In a perfect competitive market, $MC_L = \text{Wage } (W)$.

2. Profit Maximization Conditions

The firm will rationally continue hiring as long as $MRP_L > MC_L$. Profit is maximized exactly when the two equalize.

\[ MRP_L = MC_L \]
In a competitive product market where Marginal Revenue equals Price ($MR = P$):

\[ P \times MP_L = W \implies MP_L = \frac{W}{P} \]

3. The Marginal Product Curve

The Law of Diminishing Returns

Due to the Law of Diminishing Returns, the $MP_L$ eventually declines because each additional worker has less fixed capital (machines/space) to work with. The firm always hires on the downward-sloping segment of the $MP_L$ curve.

🏗️ II. Long-Run Labour Demand (LRLD)

In the long run, both Labour ($L$) and Capital ($K$) are variable. Firms choose the optimal mix of inputs based on their relative prices.

1. Cost Minimization

To maximize profits, the firm adjusts $L$ and $K$ so that the marginal cost of producing an extra unit of output is exactly the same across all inputs (the "Bang for your Buck" rule):

\[ \frac{MP_L}{W} = \frac{MP_K}{C} \]
Where $C$ is the rental cost of capital (or interest rate $r$).

2. Responses to a Wage Hike ($\uparrow W$)

🔄

Substitution Effect

Firms substitute relatively expensive labour for cheaper capital (e.g., buying self-checkout machines instead of cashiers).

📉

Scale Effect

Total production costs rise $\rightarrow$ Product price rises $\rightarrow$ Consumer demand falls $\rightarrow$ Firm shrinks and reduces use of all inputs.

🔗 III. Labour Demand Elasticities

Elasticity measures how mathematically sensitive employment is to wage changes.

1. Own-Wage Elasticity ($\eta$)

\[ \eta = \frac{\% \Delta \text{ Employment}}{\% \Delta \text{ Wage}} \]

Elastic vs. Inelastic Payroll Impact

$|\eta| > 1$ (Elastic): Total payroll ($W \times L$) falls if wages rise. (The massive loss in jobs outweighs the higher wage for survivors).

$|\eta| < 1$ (Inelastic): Total payroll rises if wages rise. (Few jobs are lost, so the higher wages dominate the total payroll).

2. The Hicks-Marshall Laws of Derived Demand

Labour demand is more elastic (firms will fire more readily when wages rise) when:

📱
1. Product Demand is Highly Elastic

(Scale Effect) If consumers easily boycott expensive products, any wage-driven price hike destroys sales and jobs massively.

🤖
2. Other Factors Are Easily Substituted

(Substitution Effect) If tech/AI exists to do the job, the firm switches instantly.

📉
3. Supply of Other Factors is Highly Elastic

If buying machines doesn't artificially inflate the price of machines, firms will substitute smoothly.

🍰
4. Labour is a Large Share of Total Costs

If wages are 80% of your expenses, a small wage hike is devastating. If they are 2%, you don't care.

🇪🇺 IV. Empirical Examples: EU Context

1. The Payroll Tax Wedge

The Tax Wedge Distortion

Taxes create a massive difference between what the firm actually pays (Gross Cost) and what the worker receives in their pocket (Net Wage). This "wedge" destroys mutually beneficial trades, reducing total employment in the EU compared to less-taxed regions.

2. Automation Threshold: Germany vs. Czechia

🇩🇪

Germany (€46/hr)

High Incentive to Automate

Wage is safely above the automation cost (€30/hr). The Substitution Effect dominates. The long-run labour demand is highly elastic because machines are cheaper.

🇨🇿

Czechia (€18.5/hr)

Lower Incentive to Automate

Wage is below the automation cost. Firms stick with human labour because the fixed costs of robots aren't justified yet.

💡 Exam Tip : High Wages & Elasticity

Conclusion: High-wage countries naturally have more elastic long-run labour demand. Any further wage hike in Germany immediately triggers automation, whereas a similar hike in Czechia might not cross the "automation threshold" yet.
