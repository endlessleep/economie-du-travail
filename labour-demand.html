Labour Market Dynamics & Equilibrium

🏭 I. Labour Demand Theory (\( L^d \))

Labour demand is a derived demand—it depends entirely on the demand for the product the labour produces.

1. The Slope of the Demand Curve

The curve is downward sloping due to two critical effects:

  • Substitution Effect: When wages rise, firms replace expensive labour with other resources (like machines/AI).
  • Scale Effect:
    1. Wage \( \uparrow \) \( \rightarrow \) Production Costs \( \uparrow \).
    2. Product Price \( \uparrow \) \( \rightarrow \) Quantity demanded by consumers \( \downarrow \).
    3. Firms scale down production \( \rightarrow \) Use of all inputs (including labour) \( \downarrow \).

Graph: The Downward Sloping Demand Curve

2. Shifts in Labour Demand

A change in wage causes a movement along the curve. A shift of the entire curve is caused by external shocks:

  • Product Demand: If the final product becomes popular, \( L^d \) shifts right.
  • Price of Other Resources: Changes in the cost of capital/machinery.
  • Technology: Shifts depending on whether tech complements or replaces workers.

Graph: Shifts in Labour Demand

3. Industry vs. Market Demand

🏭

Industry Demand

The sum of \( L^d \) curves for all firms producing the same output (e.g., all car manufacturers).

🌐

Market Demand

The sum of \( L^d \) from all industries that hire that specific type of labour (e.g., all companies needing an accountant).

Graph: Long-Run vs. Short-Run Demand

📈 II. Market Labour Supply (\( L^s \))

The market supply curve is upward sloping. As wages rise in a specific market:

  • Intensive margin: Current workers choose to work more hours.
  • Extensive margin (Mobility): Workers from other markets switch to this one.
  • Participation: Inactive individuals (not in the labour force) choose to enter the market.

Graph: The Upward Sloping Supply Curve

Shifts in Supply

The entire Supply curve can shift left or right due to:

  • Alternative Opportunities: Changes in wages in other competing markets.
  • Preferences: Changes in worker tastes (e.g., strong preference for remote work).
  • Demography: Aging populations or major migration flows.

Graph: Shifts in Labour Supply

⚖️ III. Equilibrium & Firm Behavior

1. Market vs. Individual Firm

The Market determines the equilibrium wage at the intersection of aggregate \( L^s \) and \( L^d \).
For an Individual Firm in a perfectly competitive market, labour supply is perfectly elastic (horizontal). The firm is too small to influence the market price; it is a "wage taker".

2. Shifts in Equilibrium (Comparative Statics)

When the market is shocked, both wages (\( W \)) and employment (\( L \)) adjust to find the new crossing point:

Demand Increase: \( W \uparrow \), \( L \uparrow \)
Demand Decrease: \( W \downarrow \), \( L \downarrow \)
Supply Increase: \( W \downarrow \), \( L \uparrow \)
Supply Decrease: \( W \uparrow \), \( L \downarrow \)

🇪🇺 IV. The "EU Reality": Disequilibrium & Institutions

The Neoclassical Model assumes perfect flexibility. However, in Europe, non-market forces (laws, unions, regulations) often hold wages above the market-clearing level, potentially causing persistent, structural unemployment.

Where the Textbook Model Struggles

1. Wage Rigidity (Collective Bargaining)

Due to Collective Bargaining Agreements with strong unions, wages do not drop easily even when demand falls. Because wages are "sticky downwards," the market cannot clear via price.
Result: Instead of lower wages for everyone, the market adjusts rapidly through lower employment (layoffs) or fewer vacancies.

2. Adjustment Costs (EPL)

High hiring and firing costs, driven by strict Employment Protection Legislation (EPL), mean firms are terrified to hire on permanent contracts. When a shock hits, they respond by freezing hiring or using extreme flexibility via temporary contracts rather than adjusting wages.

3. Skill Mismatch

The simple model assumes all labour is homogeneous. In reality, firms often have high vacancies but cannot hire because the unemployed lack the specific required skills (Essentially, there are multiple disconnected \( L^d \) and \( L^s \) curves for different skills).

Graph: Impact of Collective Bargaining

Notice how holding the wage above equilibrium \( W^* \) creates a direct gap between \( L^s \) and \( L^d \) (Unemployment).

💡 Exam Tip : Flexible vs Rigid

When comparing the US and European labour markets:
- The US absorbs shocks mainly through wage flexibility (wages drop, but people keep working).
- The EU absorbs shocks mainly through employment flexibility / unemployment (wages stay high due to rigidity, but people lose their jobs).
Keep this distinction clear in your essays!
