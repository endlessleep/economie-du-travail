8. Discrimination Theories and Gender

⚖️ I. Definition of Discrimination in Labour Markets

Discrimination is a central issue in labour economics because it leads to market inefficiencies and profound social inequalities. Economically speaking, discrimination exists when workers with the exact same productive capacities are treated differently on the basis of the demographic group to which they belong.

This demographic group affiliation can be based on gender, race, age, nationality, religion, sexual preference, disability, and more. When the labour market fails to reward identical human capital equally, we observe two main outcomes:

  • Wage discrimination: Two individuals with the same productivity receive different wages.
  • Employment segregation: Certain groups are systematically excluded from or concentrated in specific occupations or sectors.

🚨 Discrimination vs. Under-representation

It is crucial to distinguish between true discrimination and statistical under-representation. For instance, women have, on average, lower wages than men. However, if a country strictly applies the principle of "equal pay for equal work", the lower average female wage might simply reflect their higher concentration in lower-paid professions (e.g., care work, education). This is occupational segregation, not wage discrimination per se (although systemic factors might have forced them into those roles).

Pre-market vs. Post-market Discrimination

Inequalities do not magically appear only when a contract is signed. They are built up over a lifetime:

  • Pre-market discrimination: Occurs before entering the labour market. It includes unequal access to human capital (e.g., girls encouraged away from STEM education) or societal pressures steering groups toward specific, less-lucrative occupations.
  • Post-market (in-market) discrimination: Occurs when employers, customers, or co-workers negatively evaluate a minority group member who is already active in the labour market.

📊 Empirical Insight: Self-Reported Discrimination at Work (EU, 2021)

Heterogeneity of Discrimination

Empirical data comparing self-reported discrimination across the EU (men vs. women, native vs. non-EU origins) reveals striking structural inequalities:

  • Non-EU workers: They report the highest levels of discrimination (around 8–9%).
  • Women vs. Men: Women consistently report higher levels of discrimination across all groups.
  • Intersectionality in action:
    • Among men, discrimination is overwhelmingly linked to foreign origin. Gender discrimination is practically non-existent.
    • Among women, it is a compound effect. They face a mix of origin-based and gender-based discrimination.

Economic Analysis of these findings: Why do we observe this pattern?

  1. Gender Discrimination (Becker's model): Since it is visible almost exclusively among women, this aligns with the theory that employers or clients harbor a specific distaste (preference against) women in certain roles.
  2. Statistical Discrimination: The high discrimination rate for non-EU immigrants suggests employers use national origin as a proxy for unobservable productivity variables (language skills, cultural fit), penalizing equally competent individuals simply due to their group tag.
  3. Labour Market Segmentation (Crowding): Women and immigrants are heavily concentrated in precarious sectors or service jobs with high customer interaction, exposing them disproportionately to discriminatory behaviors.

💡 Exam Core Message

Discrimination is heterogeneous and multidimensional. While men primarily face origin-based barriers, women experience intersectional discrimination (gender + origin). Furthermore, standard cross-country surveys measure subjective perceptions of discrimination, heavily influenced by national culture and institutions, which is why economists rely on econometric tools (like Oaxaca-Blinder) to isolate pure discrimination.

📚 II. Economic Theories of Discrimination

Economists have modeled several mechanisms to explain why profit-maximizing firms might engage in discriminatory practices. We focus on three foundational theories.

1. Personal-Prejudice / Distaste Models (Becker, 1971)

Originally formulated to explain racial discrimination, Gary Becker’s model introduces the concept of a Discrimination Coefficient ($d$). Becker posits that discrimination is not a rational evaluation of skills, but a psychological "distaste" or prejudice. Certain market actors are willing to pay a financial cost to avoid interacting with a specific minority group.

A. Discrimination by Employers

Assume that a discriminated group (e.g., Women, $F$) and a non-discriminated group (Men, $M$) are equally productive. A prejudiced employer subjectively devalues the productivity of the female workers by the coefficient $d$.

\[ W_F = W_M - d \]

For the employer to be indifferent between hiring a man or a woman, the woman must accept a wage penalty equal to the employer's distaste $d$.

The Profit Sacrifice: A discriminatory employer will only hire women if their wage is lower enough to compensate for $d$. If women are not employed even though their market wage is lower than men's, the discriminatory employer is actively sacrificing profits to avoid them! Thus, the employer is maximizing their personal utility (profit minus distaste), not just financial profit. In a perfectly competitive market, non-discriminatory firms would hire all the underpaid women, outcompete the prejudiced firms, and eventually drive them out of business. The persistence of discrimination challenges the perfect competition assumption.

B. Discrimination by Employees

Here, the prejudice comes from the workers themselves. Male workers might have a distaste for taking orders from female managers or working alongside them. Consequently, these prejudiced men will demand a compensating wage premium to cover their "distaste" for the mixed environment.

Why would a rational employer pay higher wages to prejudiced men if they can hire equally productive women for less? Instead of paying the premium, the profit-maximizing employer will naturally segregate the firm: hiring only men or only women. Employee discrimination thus leads to total workplace segregation rather than persistent wage differentials within the same firm.

C. Discrimination by Customers

Customers might harbor prejudices and prefer to be served by a specific demographic (e.g., men in high-end finance, women in caregiving). Customers act as if the price of the good is higher when purchased from the discriminated group. To compensate, a firm would have to lower the price to maintain sales, hurting profits. Therefore, the employer will systematically offer a lower wage to the discriminated group or refuse to hire them for customer-facing roles.

Caveat: This requires the worker to be visible to the customer. This perfectly explains occupational segregation in high-contact service sectors.

2. Statistical Discrimination

Unlike Becker’s model, statistical discrimination is not driven by hatred or distaste, but by imperfect information and the desire to minimize risk and hiring costs.

When screening applicants, employers cannot perfectly observe the true, idiosyncratic productivity of an individual. Gathering absolute proof of an applicant's future performance is too expensive. Therefore, employers use observable characteristics (gender, race, age) as cheap proxies (signals) to estimate expected productivity, basing their decisions on past experiences, historical group statistics, or entrenched biases.

Empirical Observation: Overlapping productivity distributions of two groups under imperfect information.

Economic Interpretation: The Intersection Penalty

The graph illustrates two overlapping normal distributions. Let's assume Group 2 (e.g., native men) has a historically higher average productivity than Group 1 (e.g., young mothers) due to systematic uninterrupted career paths. However, the distributions heavily overlap.

At the intersection point, a specific candidate from Group 1 has the exact same actual productivity as a candidate from Group 2. But because the employer cannot see this specific point, they anchor their offer to the group average. As a result, the individual from Group 1 is heavily penalized, offered a lower wage, or not hired at all.

🚨 Self-Fulfilling Prophecy (Crucial Mechanism)

Statistical discrimination is insidious because it creates a vicious cycle. If women know that employers expect them to drop out of the workforce, and therefore offer them lower-paying, dead-end jobs, women have reduced incentives to invest heavily in specialized human capital. Their average group productivity then does fall over time, perfectly "validating" the employer's initial bias.

3. Crowding Hypothesis and Dual Labour Markets

This theory argues that discrimination manifests as barriers to mobility, artificially splitting the economy into a Dual Labour Market:

  • Primary Sector: High wages, stable employment, excellent working conditions, and clear career advancement tracks (Internal Labour Markets).
  • Secondary Sector: Low-wage, unstable, precarious contracts, and poor working conditions.

Minorities and women are often "tagged" as undesirable for the primary sector (due to either distaste or statistical discrimination) and find it nearly impossible to cross the barrier, creating the "Glass ceiling" effect.

The Crowding Effect: How occupational segregation suppresses wages independently of individual productivity.

The Mechanism of Crowding

Because women are systematically excluded from the primary sector (e.g., executive, heavy industry, elite tech), they are forced to crowd into a limited number of occupations in the secondary sector (e.g., retail, care, admin).

The Microeconomic Result: This massive influx drastically shifts the Labour Supply curve in the secondary sector to the right, generating a structural excess supply. This mechanically crashes the equilibrium wage to a low level ($W_L$). Meanwhile, the primary sector remains artificially protected from competition, keeping its labour supply scarce and its wages high ($W_H$). The wage gap here isn't caused by individual productivity flaws, but entirely by the market structure and forced sector allocation.

💶 III. The Gender Wage Gap (GWG / GPG)

The Gender Wage Gap (also known as the Gender Pay Gap) is the universal metric used to quantify these theoretical inequalities. It is defined as the percentage difference between the average gross hourly earnings of male and female employees.

\[ GWG = \frac{\bar{W}_M - \bar{W}_F}{\bar{W}_M} \times 100 = \left( 1 - \frac{\bar{W}_F}{\bar{W}_M} \right) \times 100 \]

Where $\bar{W}_M$ is the average male wage and $\bar{W}_F$ is the average female wage.

Trends in the Unadjusted GPG (Czech Republic & EU)

Data from Eurostat reveals significant structural dynamics. The unadjusted GPG in Czechia hovered around a massive 22–23% between 2011 and 2015, steadily declined to ~15% by 2021, and then crept back up, stabilizing around 18–19% by 2024. In a cross-European context (2024), the EU-27 average sits at 11%, but heterogeneity is extreme:

  • Low GPG: Luxembourg, Belgium (near 0% or negative).
  • Medium GPG: Spain, France, Netherlands (10–12%).
  • High GPG: Germany, Austria, Czechia, Estonia (15–19%).

🚨 The Trap of the "Unadjusted" GPG

The unadjusted GPG is just a raw mathematical average. It includes differences in education choices, accumulated experience, hours worked, and occupation sectors. It DOES NOT measure pure discrimination! Taking a low GPG as a sign of absolute equality is a dangerous analytical error, as it often masks a deeper phenomenon known as Selection Bias.

🔍 IV. The Illusion of Equality: Selection Bias & Employment Gaps

Why do countries like Italy, Greece, and Romania boast an impressively low Gender Pay Gap, while economic powerhouses like Germany and Czechia display huge disparities? The answer lies in the Gender Employment Gap (GEG).

Gender Employment Gap 2024 (Czechia vs EU-27)

Region Male Employment Rate Female Employment Rate
Czechia 88.4% 75.8%
EU-27 Avg 80.8% 70.8%

The GEG measures who is actually working. The data shows a powerful, somewhat counter-intuitive negative correlation (-0.29) between the Gender Wage Gap and the Gender Employment Gap.

Understanding Heckman's Selection Bias Mechanism

In countries with severe structural barriers, poor childcare infrastructure, or conservative social norms (like parts of Southern Europe), a large proportion of women are entirely excluded from the labour market. The women who do manage to overcome these barriers and secure employment are overwhelmingly the hyper-educated, elite professionals (doctors, lawyers, executives).

Because the low-skilled women are out of the equation (their wage is zero, so they aren't counted in the GPG), the statistical average wage of the remaining working women is artificially inflated. This crushes the GPG toward zero, creating an illusion of equality.

Conversely, in Czechia, female participation is robustly high. Millions of women with average or lower-tier skills are currently employed (many in secondary sectors due to crowding), which drags the average female wage down, exposing a high GPG of 18.5%.

📉 Case Study: Hunt (2002) & East Germany

Jennifer Hunt's analysis of post-reunification East Germany (1990-1994) offers perfect proof. In just four years, the Gender Pay Gap fell dramatically by 10 percentage points! A cause for celebration? No. Hunt demonstrated that almost half of this statistical "improvement" occurred simply because low-skilled women were fired en masse during the transition, vanishing from the data pool.

🎓 V. Deconstructing the Causes: Why does the Gap persist?

If we strip away statistical illusions, what genuinely causes the gap in countries like Czechia?

1. Education and the Human Capital Paradox

According to classic Human Capital theory, wages reward education. Following the 2000 Bologna Process, tertiary education exploded across Eastern Europe. Let's look at the Czech metrics for tertiary education (aged 25-64):

  • 2004: Men 13.9% vs Women 10.8% (Men more educated)
  • 2024: Men 24.0% vs Women 31.1% (Women significantly more educated!)

The Paradox: The education gap hasn't just closed; it has totally reversed in favor of women! Yet, the massive wage gap remains. Why? Because Human Capital is not homogeneous. We must look at Field of Study Segregation.

In Czechia (2022 stats):

  • Women dominate low-yielding sectors: Education (82%), Health/Welfare (78%), Humanities (70%).
  • Men unconditionally dominate high-yield sectors: ICT (83% men), Engineering/Manufacturing (68% men).

Because patriarchal norms and anticipated discrimination funnel women away from lucrative STEM fields before they even enter the job market, educational segregation forms the bedrock of eventual occupational crowding.

2. Age, Work Experience, and Career Interruptions

Women's age/earnings profiles are typically much flatter than men's. The primary culprit is the massive human capital depreciation caused by career interruptions (maternity leave). While men accumulate continuous, unbroken work experience, women lose exponential compounded wage growth during their absences, creating an unbridgeable gap.

🛠️ VI. The Oaxaca-Blinder Decomposition: The Ultimate Tool

To mathematically untangle what portion of the Gender Wage Gap is justified by objective market logic (education, experience, sector) and what portion is unjustifiable (discrimination), economists Ronald Oaxaca and Alan Blinder (1973) standardized a powerful econometric technique. By running separate Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) Mincer wage regressions for men and women, we can decompose the gap.

Oaxaca-Blinder Formula

\[ \ln \bar{W}^M - \ln \bar{W}^F = \underbrace{\left( \bar{X}^M - \bar{X}^F \right)\hat{\beta}^M}_{\text{Endowment effect (explained part)}} + \underbrace{\bar{X}^F \left( \hat{\beta}^M - \hat{\beta}^F \right)}_{\text{Remuneration effect (unexplained part)}} \]

The Two Pillars of Decomposition

  • The Endowment Effect (Explained part): This represents the wage gap that would exist even if women were perfectly treated like men. It is the hypothetical wage difference driven purely by the fact that men and women have different vectors of characteristics ($X$)—such as men having more years of uninterrupted experience or working in higher-paying sectors.
  • The Remuneration Effect (Unexplained part): This measures the core inequality. It represents the gap generated purely because the market pays differently ($\beta$) for the exact same characteristics. A woman with a master's degree in a specific field receives a lower return on her degree ($\hat{\beta}^F$) than a man with the identical degree ($\hat{\beta}^M$).

Czech Republic Decomposition Analysis (2005–2016)

When running a basic Mincer equation (controlling only for education and experience), the model successfully explains almost 0% to 2% of the gap. The overwhelming majority of the 20%+ wage gap falls into the "Unexplained" category.

When economists dramatically expanded the control variables to include occupation type, supervisory position, firm size, contract type, industry, region, and children, the explanatory power shot up: addressing 17% of the total GPG in 2005 and rising to 31% by 2016.

The Adjusted Pay Gap: A Persistent Reality

In Eurostat terminology:

  • Unadjusted form: The total raw GPG.
  • Adjusted form: The total GPG minus the explained part.

When we look at the Adjusted GPG across Europe (e.g., controlling for all variables, comparing a woman vs a man with identical resumes in the exact same firm sector), the gap strictly shrinks, confirming that human capital segregation is real.

However, the Adjusted GPG never reaches zero. In Czechia, it dropped from 14.5% to 10.8% in 2018, but a massive 10.8% gap remains. This highly persistent, unadjusted remnant captures unobservable variables (negotiation aggressiveness, nuanced motivation differences) but stands as the strongest, most undeniable quantitative proxy for systemic market discrimination.

💡 Exam Synthesis

"The adjusted gender pay gap isolates differences in returns to characteristics, but cannot be interpreted as pure discrimination due to unobserved heterogeneity. Nevertheless, its persistent failure to close despite women overtaking men in raw educational attainment proves that systemic issues—such as occupational crowding, statistical discrimination biases, and institutional penalties for caregiving—are the true drivers of the modern labour crisis."
