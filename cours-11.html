11. Active Labour Market Policies (ALMP)

🧠 I. Understanding Long-Term Unemployment (LTU)

The core objective of Active Labour Market Policies (ALMPs) is to increase employment opportunities for job seekers, return the unemployed to employment as fast as possible, and improve the matching between vacancies and unemployed individuals. These interventions target specific vulnerable groups: the long-term unemployed, youth, older workers, low-skilled, and the disabled.

Beyond Simple Unemployment Rates

Not all unemployment is equal. What matters is not only how many people are unemployed, but how long they remain unemployed. Long-term unemployment (LTU), defined as unemployment lasting more than 12 months, represents a fundamentally different and more problematic phenomenon than short-term unemployment.

While short-term unemployment can be interpreted as part of normal labour market dynamics (frictional matching), long-term unemployment signals a structural breakdown in the matching process. It suggests that a portion of the unemployed population is no longer effectively integrated into the labour market.

Level vs. Structure

There is a critical distinction between the LTU rate (as a % of the labour force) and the share of LTU in total unemployment. A country might have low overall unemployment but a high share of long-term unemployed. This means while few people are unemployed, those who are remain trapped for extended periods. A low unemployment rate does not guarantee a well-functioning labour market if unemployment is highly persistent.

The Structural Trap

The underlying mechanism of LTU is cumulative disadvantage: skills deteriorate over time, work habits weaken, motivation declines (the "discouraged worker effect"), and employers perceive long-term unemployed workers as less desirable. This creates a self-reinforcing cycle where the probability of re-employment decreases the longer an individual is unemployed.

ALMP as the Structural Antidote

Countries with higher participation in ALMPs tend to exhibit lower levels of long-term unemployment. Unlike passive policies (such as unemployment benefits) that only provide income support, ALMPs actively reintegrate individuals through:

  • Improving matching efficiency (job search assistance)
  • Enhancing employability (training and requalification)
  • Maintaining labour market attachment (public works, subsidized jobs)
  • Reducing discouragement and inactivity

Core Economic Takeaway: Long-term unemployment is a structural and persistent problem that cannot be solved by economic growth alone. It requires targeted and active policy interventions. ALMP effectiveness must be judged by its ability to reduce LTU, not just the overall unemployment rate.

🛠️ II. The Tools of Active Labour Market Policy

ALMPs encompass all forms of intervention brought forth by institutions aimed at enhancing employment and labour market participation. The main tools operate through distinct economic mechanisms:

Main Forms of ALMP

  • Public Employment Services (PES - úřad práce): Public institutions offer direct help in the job search. This reduces search costs and enhances efficacy, acting as an intermediary to pass job offers to suitable workers.
  • Training: Providing training or promoting on-the-job training programs to equip unemployed workers with useful skills, directly targeting the human capital deficit and enhancing employability.
  • Subsidized new working places & supported employment (SUPM): Monetary incentives to firms that hire workers, often aimed at specific categories of disadvantaged workers to compensate for perceived risks or lower productivity.
  • Direct public employment programs (VPP - veřejně prospěšné práce): Public institutions directly hire workers to create ad hoc positions. These are temporary, aiming to provide direct working experience that maintains labour market attachment.
  • Start-up incentives: Support for new entrepreneurship (better credit conditions, fiscal discounts, counselling) to stimulate job creation from the supply side.

Passive vs Active Policies: The Expenditure Debate

💰 Understanding Labour Market Policy Expenditures

A key distinction emerges in labour market budgets: the difference between Passive Labour Market Policies (PLMP) and ALMP. Passive policies (unemployment benefits) provide income support, while active policies improve employment outcomes. The data shows a persistent tension: governments must balance short-term welfare protection (preventing poverty) with long-term labour market efficiency.

🧠 The Structural Imbalance & Trade-offs

The structure of unemployment benefits introduces a key behavioural mechanism: the trade-off between income security and job search incentives. A high initial replacement rate reduces financial stress, but if benefits remain too high for too long, they increase the reservation wage and prolong unemployment duration. This is why benefits typically decline over time.

🎯 The Principle of Complementarity

ALMPs and unemployment benefits must be coordinated. Participation in ALMP programs can reduce the negative (disincentive) effects of generous benefits by maintaining job search activity. However, if ALMPs only mimic passive support by providing compensation without improving prospects, they may inadvertently increase reservation wages. Policy design matters more than raw spending.

📐 III. Theory: Equilibrium Unemployment & The Layard-Nickell Model

To understand why ALMPs are necessary, we must look at the structural nature of unemployment. The Layard & Nickell (1986) model illustrates that unemployment can persist even when the market is technically "in equilibrium."

📉 Graph Analysis: The Layard-Nickell Model

⚙️ The Two Core Forces:

  • The Employment Schedule (Labour Demand): The downward-sloping curve. When real wages increase, firms reduce employment (higher costs mean less hiring).
  • The Wage-Setting Schedule: The upward-sloping curve. When employment is higher, labour is scarce, so firms must offer higher wages to attract workers, and worker bargaining power is stronger.

🎯 Equilibrium Point A:

  • Intersection where firms are willing to hire at that wage, and workers accept that wage. There is no pressure for wages or employment to change.

⚠️ Involuntary Unemployment ($u_0$):

  • The vertical line on the far right is the Full Employment line. Point A lies to the left of it.
  • The horizontal distance between Equilibrium A and Full Employment is involuntary unemployment. Workers are willing to work at the current wage, but firms do not want to hire more.

🧠 Deep Intuition (Exam Critical):

  • Unemployment here is built into the structure of the labour market. It is the result of how wages are set relative to firms' willingness to hire. Reducing this unemployment requires structural policies (ALMPs) to shift these curves, not just economic growth.

🔄 IV. The Modified Model: Evaluating Gross vs. Net Effects

We modify the Layard-Nickell model to explicitly incorporate ALMP by distinguishing between regular employment and programme participation.

📉 Graph Analysis: Decomposing the Labour Force

⚙️ The Three Components:

  • e (Regular employment): working in standard jobs.
  • u (Open unemployment): actively searching for a job.
  • r (Programme participation): enrolled in training or job creation programmes.

⚠️ The Gross Effect of ALMP:

  • If individuals move from open unemployment ($u$) into programmes ($r$), the measured unemployment rate falls. The vertical R-line shifts left.
  • This is the Gross Effect: unemployment decreases simply because people are reclassified, not necessarily because they found a regular job ($e$).

🎯 Core Economic Insight:

  • Reducing measured unemployment is not the same as increasing employment. If ALMPs only move people into programmes without improving their chances of finding a regular job, they do not solve the underlying problem. The success of ALMPs must be assessed based on their "Net Effect" — their structural impact on transitions into stable, regular employment.

🧩 V. The Indirect Effects of ALMP

The true net effect of ALMPs comes from how they alter the structural curves of the labour market. These indirect effects can be positive (increasing employment) or negative (decreasing employment).

1. Matching Efficiency

📉 Mechanism: Faster Hiring & Lower Wage Pressure

ALMPs (training, job search assistance) improve how efficiently workers and jobs meet.

  • Wage-setting shifts down/right: Because firms fill vacancies faster, they no longer need to offer high wages to attract workers. Upward wage pressure is reduced.
  • Labour demand shifts up/right: Because hiring is faster and less costly, firms are encouraged to open more vacancies.
  • Exam Logic: ALMP → better matching → wage-setting ↓ and labour demand ↑ → Regular employment ↑. The wage effect is theoretically ambiguous.

2. Competition Effects

📉 Mechanism: Increasing Labour Supply Pressure

By preventing the short-term unemployed from giving up, and by bringing inactive people back into the search pool, ALMPs increase the number of active job seekers.

  • More active job seekers intensify competition for jobs. Workers have weaker bargaining power.
  • Wage-setting shifts down: Firms can hire at a lower cost because workers accept lower wages.
  • Exam Logic: ALMP → more active job seekers → competition ↑ → wage-setting ↓ → wages ↓ and Regular employment ↑. This increases employment by making labour cheaper, not necessarily by improving productivity.

3. Productivity Effects

📉 Mechanism: The Ambiguous Outcome of Better Skills

If ALMP successfully increases workers' productivity, two opposing forces emerge:

  • Scale Effect (+): Higher productivity means lower unit costs, so firms expand production and demand more labour (shifts right).
  • Substitution Effect (-): Higher productivity means firms need fewer workers to produce the same output (shifts left).

Furthermore, more productive workers demand higher wages (wage-setting shifts up). Exam Logic: ALMP → productivity ↑ → Scale vs Substitution effect + Wage pressure ↑ → Final effect on employment is ambiguous. However, there are likely welfare gains from higher output.

4. Reduced Welfare Losses for the Unemployed

📉 Mechanism: The Unintended Consequence of Comfort

If participation in ALMP provides good compensation and reduces the risk of unemployability, the "cost" of being unemployed goes down.

  • When the hardship of unemployment decreases, workers become less desperate and raise their reservation wage.
  • Wage-setting shifts up/left: Firms must offer higher wages to hire.
  • Exam Logic: ALMP → welfare loss ↓ → reservation wage ↑ → wage-setting ↑ → wages ↑ and Regular employment ↓. This highlights the fundamental trade-off between social protection and labour market efficiency.

5. Deadweight and Substitution Effects

📉 Mechanism: Why Job Creation Can Be Illusory

This is a massive issue in practice (representing 70-90% of gross jobs created according to Calmfors, 1994).

  • Deadweight Loss: The firm receives a subsidy to hire someone they would have hired anyway. No real change, just a money transfer.
  • Substitution Effect: The firm hires a subsidized worker but replaces a non-subsidized worker. Total employment doesn't increase, it is just reallocated.
  • Result: Labour demand for non-participants decreases, leading to lower wages and a much smaller (or negative) net employment effect. Policies must ensure additionality — creating jobs that would not exist otherwise.

🔬 VI. Empirical Evaluation: Do ALMPs Actually Work?

Measuring the effectiveness of ALMP involves moving from theory to practice. Studies are broadly split into Macroeconomic studies (aggregate LM metrics) and Experimental/Micro studies (comparing treated participants vs a control group). In practice, defining a pure control group is extremely difficult because most registered unemployed eventually enter a program.

The Czech Context: SUPM and VPP (2015–2019)

The "Nové pracovní příležitosti" project evaluated two major Czech programs: SUPM (Subsidized jobs) and VPP (Public works). The target groups were distinct:

  • SUPM: Higher share of women (64%), mostly secondary educated, mean age 40. Focus on placing people into private sector roles.
  • VPP: Even gender split, heavily primary (46%) and secondary educated, older mean age (45). More often targeted at people with cumulated disadvantages (disabled, immigrants, homeless).

Logistic regressions evaluating the probability of being employed 18 months after entering the program found positive effects for females, higher education, and longer duration of support. However, it found negative effects for the disabled and minorities, showing the severe limits of standard ALMP tools against deep systemic barriers.

What Empirical Evidence Reveals (The Real-World Exam Takeaway)

1. The Decaying Employment Effect

Graphs tracking participants before and after program entry show a sharp increase in employment immediately after the program. However, this effect weakens significantly after 12 months. ALMP may improve short-term transitions, but it does not always guarantee durable integration. Subsidized jobs or public works often end, pushing the participant back into unemployment if no real human capital was gained.

2. Extreme Target Group Heterogeneity

ALMPs do not work the same for everyone. For youth, programs are highly effective because they just lack experience. For parents, lack of childcare is a barrier standard ALMPs can't fix. For older workers, age discrimination heavily dampens retraining efficacy. For socially excluded groups, ALMP alone is insufficient without broader social policy integration. One size does not fit all.

💡 Exam-Ready Conclusion

Empirical evidence suggests that ALMPs can have positive effects, especially in the short run, but their effectiveness is limited and highly heterogeneous.

They work best for participants relatively close to the labour market (e.g., youth needing job-search assistance). They are far less effective for groups facing cumulative disadvantages (disabled, older workers). Furthermore, deadweight and substitution effects often drastically reduce the true net impact of the policy.

ALMPs are useful activation tools, but they are not a universal panacea for unemployment. Their success depends on precise programme design, strict targeting, and their ability to forge stable, long-term regular employment rather than temporary statistical illusions.
