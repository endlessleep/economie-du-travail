Labour Market Frictions

🏃 I. Frictions on the Employee Side

In a perfect market, the "Law of One Price" suggests workers with identical skills receive identical wages. Frictions violate this.

1. Costly Mobility & Job Search

Mobility is not free. Moving costs, emotional costs, and the time spent searching for a new job mean that the Labour Supply curve facing a firm is not horizontal, but upward sloping.

The Cost of Searching

  • High Mobility Costs = Steeper $L^s$ curve (lower elasticity).
  • Search Costs: Finding a "good" job takes time. This explains why unemployment exists even when jobs are available and why wages grow with job tenure (better matches are rewarded).

Graph: Costly Mobility Supply Curve

2. Monopsony Power

A Monopsony occurs when there is only one buyer (employer) in a market. Because the firm faces the entire upward-sloping market supply curve, to hire one more worker, they must raise the wage for all workers.

The Monopsony Trap

The Marginal Cost of Labour ($MC_L$) is strictly higher than the Wage ($W$).
Result: The monopsonist hires fewer workers ($E_m$) and pays a lower wage ($W_m$) than a perfectly competitive market would.

3. Minimum Wage in a Monopsony

Unlike in a competitive market (where a minimum wage usually causes unemployment), a minimum wage in a monopsony can actually increase both wages and employment by making the $MC_L$ curve horizontal at the mandated wage level.

🏢 II. Frictions on the Employer Side: Quasi-Fixed Costs

Firms face Quasi-fixed costs: costs associated with the number of workers ($M$) rather than the hours worked ($H$).

1. Labour Investments

📝

Hiring Costs

Advertising, screening, and interviewing. Firms use "credentials" (degree, experience) as signals to minimize these costs.

🛠️

Training Costs

General: Increases productivity anywhere (e.g. Excel). Workers pay via lower starting wages.
Specific: Increases productivity only in current firm. Costs are shared to ensure long-term stability.

2. The M vs. H Trade-Off

Firms can increase output by hiring more people ($M$) or asking current people to work more hours ($H$).

\[ Q = f(M, H) \]

Optimization: Firms balance the quasi-fixed costs of a new person against the overtime premium costs of extra hours.

🇪🇺 III. Adjustment Costs & EU Context

1. Asymmetric Adjustment

Sticky Employment

Firms do not adjust employment instantly. Firing costs (legal protections, severance) are often much higher than hiring costs, especially in the EU.

Consequence: In a recession, firms cut Hours ($H$) immediately, but delay cutting Employment ($M$) — utilizing Short-time work schemes.

2. The COVID-19 Legacy & Happiness

  • Short-time Work: Protected jobs during the pandemic but may have slowed down "productive reallocation" (keeping workers in dying industries).
  • Worker Well-being: Emerging evidence suggests "happy workers" are more productive. Happiness boosts effort and output without sacrificing quality.

⚖️ IV. Policy Impacts

1. Overtime Premiums

Increasing the cost of extra hours ($ME_H$) leads to a Substitution Effect (hire more people, reduce hours) but also a Scale Effect (total costs rise, so both $M$ and $H$ might eventually fall).

2. Productivity Paradox

Falling working hours in the EU do not necessarily mean lower output; technological advancement often offsets the reduction in time.
